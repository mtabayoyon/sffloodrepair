Fire Damage Insurance Claims

Fire claims are large and complex — covering structure, belongings, living expenses, and the smoke and water damage that come with the fire. How fire coverage works and how to claim the full scope.

Read8 min
UpdatedJuly 2026
ByAllied Restoration
ForHomeowners · Renters · Managers

A fire is traumatic, and the insurance claim that follows is often large and complex — covering not just the structure, but your belongings, additional living expenses, and secondary smoke and water damage. Fire claims are usually covered, but the size and complexity create their own pitfalls. Here is how fire damage coverage works and how to navigate the claim.

SECTION 01Fire Is Broadly Covered — With Nuances

Good news first: fire is a covered peril under virtually every standard homeowners and renters policy. Whether the fire came from cooking, electrical faults, wildfire, or a candle, the damage is typically covered. But fire claims have moving parts most water claims do not:

  • Dwelling coverage — the structure itself
  • Personal property coverage — your belongings (often at replacement cost or actual cash value — know which you have)
  • Additional Living Expenses (ALE) — hotel, meals, and costs while you cannot live at home
  • Secondary damage — smoke, soot, and the water used to extinguish the fire
Wildfire Is Still Fire

In California, homeowners worry wildfire is excluded. Standard policies generally cover wildfire damage as a fire peril. The bigger issue is availability and cost of coverage in high-risk areas — but if you have a policy, fire (including wildfire) is typically covered.

SECTION 02The Hidden Scope: Smoke & Water Come With the Fire

One of the most under-claimed aspects of a fire is everything the fire did not directly burn. Smoke and soot travel throughout the building, embedding in walls, contents, and the HVAC system. The water used to fight the fire causes its own damage and mold risk. A proper fire claim includes all of it — structure, smoke, soot, water, and contents — not just the visibly charred area.

Don't Under-Scope the Claim

Homeowners often settle a fire claim covering only the burned room, then discover smoke odor throughout the house and water damage in the floors below. Document the FULL extent — smoke, soot, and water in every affected area — before settling.

SECTION 03Documentation & the Contents Inventory

  1. Ensure safety first — do not enter until authorities clear the structure
  2. Document everything — photos and video of all damage before any cleanup
  3. Report immediately — fire claims should be opened fast
  4. Build a contents inventory — a room-by-room list of damaged belongings with approximate value; this is often the most tedious but valuable part of a fire claim
  5. Track ALE expenses — keep every receipt for lodging, meals, and extra costs while displaced
  6. Secure the property — board-up and tarping are required mitigation and are covered

SECTION 04Why Professional Help Matters on Fire Claims

Fire claims are large enough that thorough documentation directly affects the settlement. A restoration company documents the full scope — structural, smoke, soot, water, and contents — in the insurer's format, handles emergency board-up and securing, and works directly with your adjuster. For big losses, this scope documentation is often the difference between a claim that rebuilds your home and one that falls short.
