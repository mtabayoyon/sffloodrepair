Frequently Asked Questions — Mold Remediation

Published February 10, 2025 · By Michael T., Allied Restoration Company

Home › Articles › Mold Remediation FAQ
Mold remediation is the process of removing mold contamination from structures, systems, and contents to restore them to a safe condition. The process includes assessing contamination, containing the affected area to prevent spread, physically removing mold-contaminated materials, cleaning surfaces with antimicrobial agents, and verifying successful remediation with post-testing. Remediation is different from mold removal — because mold spores are always present in the environment, the goal is to return mold levels to normal background levels, not to eliminate all mold permanently.
Common signs of mold include a musty or earthy odor (similar to dirty socks or wet paper), visible dark spots on walls, ceilings, or grout, water stains that have not been dried properly, unexplained respiratory symptoms or allergy-like reactions that improve when you leave the home, and a history of water damage or elevated humidity. The only way to confirm mold and assess the extent of contamination is through professional inspection and air sampling.
Mold can begin growing within 24 to 72 hours of a moisture intrusion event under the right conditions — sufficient moisture, organic material (drywall, wood, carpet), and temperatures between 40°F and 100°F. In San Francisco's climate, mold can establish in as little as 48 hours. This is why Allied Restoration emphasizes immediate response to water damage events.
Small surface mold on non-porous surfaces (like tile grout or a bathroom caulk line under 10 square feet) can sometimes be addressed with EPA-registered disinfectants. However, mold on porous materials — drywall, wood framing, insulation, carpet — cannot be cleaned. These materials must be removed and replaced. Additionally, attempting to clean mold without proper containment can spread spores throughout the home, significantly worsening the problem. For any mold affecting more than 10 square feet, or any mold in wall cavities, HVAC systems, or attic spaces, professional remediation is required.
Mold testing (inspection) is the assessment phase — air sampling, surface sampling, moisture mapping, and visual inspection to determine whether mold is present, what types exist, and how extensive the contamination is. Mold remediation is the remediation phase — the actual physical removal and cleaning of mold contamination. They are two separate services. Allied Restoration offers both, and we recommend third-party post-remediation testing (clearance testing) after remediation to verify the work was successful.

If mold resulted from a covered water damage event (sudden pipe burst, for example), most policies will cover remediation as part of the water damage claim. Mold resulting from long-term neglect, gradual leaks, or flooding (without flood insurance) is typically not covered. Allied Restoration works directly with insurance carriers and can help document mold damage in the context of a covered water loss claim.
A typical residential mold remediation project takes 3 to 7 days, depending on the scope. This includes containment setup (1 day), physical remediation (1–3 days), and post-remediation verification testing (1–2 days waiting for lab results). Larger commercial projects or severe contamination can take 2–4 weeks. After remediation is complete and clearance testing confirms success, reconstruction of removed materials begins.
Black mold typically refers to Stachybotrys chartarum, a toxigenic mold species that produces mycotoxins. While all mold should be remediated, Stachybotrys requires especially careful handling. It typically grows in areas that have been wet for extended periods and appears dark greenish-black. That said, not all black-colored mold is Stachybotrys, and not all dangerous mold is black. Laboratory analysis of air samples or surface swabs is the only way to accurately identify mold species. Allied Restoration follows the same rigorous remediation protocols regardless of species.
The key to mold prevention is moisture control. After remediation, ensure the moisture source that caused the original growth is corrected. Maintain indoor humidity below 60% (ideally 30–50%) using dehumidifiers if needed. Ensure good ventilation in bathrooms, kitchens, and laundry rooms. Address any water intrusion events immediately — call Allied Restoration within 2 hours of discovering water damage to prevent mold from establishing.
