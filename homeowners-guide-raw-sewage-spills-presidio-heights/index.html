Common Causes of Sewage Spills

Next Steps

Sometimes, there's a single cause for a sewage spill; other times, it's a combination of different factors that happen to coincide at the worst possible time. One of the most frequent causes of sewage backups in urban and suburban areas is what's known as “tree root intrusion.”

Trees planted after sewage lines were laid down can grow their roots out far enough to puncture the piping. This is a particularly notorious problem in San Francisco as the city has a number of clay pipes that are easily destroyed by tree roots, according to SF Gate.

Heavy rainstorms can also cause sewage backups. When persistent amounts of rain flood sewage lines, dragging leaves, branches, dirt and debris along with it, the flow of sewage and water can be overwhelmed, often resulting in a backup. This generally affects more than one home.

Another common yet preventable cause of sewage spills is the improper disposal of objects and fluids in sinks and toilets.

Flushing anything other than toilet paper and waste down a toilet can cause problems. Equally, pouring grease, oil and fat down sink drains can create the right conditions for water and sewage to backup and eventually overflow, according to Bay Keeper.

What to Do When Sewage Invades Your Home

The most important piece of advice for homeowners who experience a sewage spill in the home is to leave the mess to professionals. Perhaps more than any other accident, sewage spills are dangerous for untrained and unequipped people to address. With the threats to health and the risk of toxic contamination, proper safety and sanitary protocols must be followed.

Homeowners can benefit, however, from wearing thick rubber boots, breathing masks, gloves and eye protection near the site of the spill. It is also a smart idea to keep the immediate area surrounding the site as clean as possible.
