Why Emergency Water Damage Response Speed Matters

Water damage compounds rapidly. Within the first hour, water infiltrates porous building materials — drywall, insulation, wood framing, and flooring — wicking upward and outward from the point of entry. Within 24 hours, structural deterioration begins and mold growth becomes a real risk. By 48 hours, mold is actively colonizing wet materials and the scope of damage has often doubled.

This is why Allied Restoration Company maintains a 60-minute emergency response target across all four of our Bay Area offices. Every hour matters. Our dispatch team is staffed 24 hours a day, 7 days a week, 365 days a year — including Christmas and New Year's Eve.

What to Do While You Wait for Us

Do these things immediately — before our crew arrives:

  1. Call (415) 529-5637 — dispatch begins immediately
  2. Shut off water at the main valve if the source is a burst pipe or appliance
  3. Turn off electricity in affected rooms — never enter a flooded room with active electrical
  4. Move valuables to higher ground or dry areas
  5. Document with photos — photograph everything before cleanup for insurance
  6. Do not use fans or household dehumidifiers — they spread moisture, not remove it

Our Emergency Water Damage Response Process

Step 1 — Arrival

IICRC-certified crew arrives within 60 minutes. Thermal imaging camera used to locate all hidden moisture pockets. Full damage assessment documented.

Step 2 — Extraction

Truck-mounted and portable extractors remove standing water. Floor mat drying systems deployed for hardwood. Wet materials triaged for salvageability.

Step 3 — Drying Setup

LGR 6000Li dehumidifiers and Crosswind AX air movers placed in calculated positions. Negative pressure drying chambers created for wall cavities when needed.

Step 4 — Monitoring

Daily moisture readings logged with calibrated meters. IICRC S500 drying standards met before equipment removal. Complete documentation for insurance claim.

Common Emergency Water Damage Situations We Handle

  • Burst pipes — frozen pipe thaw events and pressure failures in SF Victorian plumbing
  • Appliance failures — washing machines, dishwashers, refrigerator ice lines, water heaters
  • Toilet overflow and sewage backup — Category 2 and Category 3 water damage
  • Storm flooding and roof leaks — especially during atmospheric river events
  • Fire sprinkler activation — the water damage from suppression often exceeds the fire damage
  • Slab leaks — often undetected for weeks before catastrophic failure
  • HVAC condensation line failures — common in SF's older multi-unit buildings
