Does Renters Insurance Cover Fire Damage?

After a fire, does renters insurance cover the damage? Fire is one of the most universally covered perils. What renters insurance covers, what it does not, and how to file your claim.

UpdatedJuly 2026
ByAllied Restoration
ForRenters · Tenants

After a fire, one of the first worries for renters is money: does renters insurance cover fire damage? The good news is that fire is one of the most universally covered perils. Here is what renters insurance covers after a fire, what it does not, and how to make your claim go smoothly.

SECTION 01Yes — Fire Is a Covered Peril

Fire is a standard covered peril on virtually all renters insurance policies. If a fire damages or destroys your belongings, your renters policy typically reimburses you for them. This covers fire from most causes — accidental fires, electrical fires, kitchen fires, and even fires that start in another unit and spread to yours.

Three Ways Renters Insurance Helps After a Fire

1) Personal property — reimburses your damaged belongings. 2) Loss of use — pays for temporary housing and extra living costs while you cannot live there. 3) Liability — if you accidentally caused a fire that damaged others' property, it may help cover that.

SECTION 02What Renters Insurance Covers

  • Your belongings — furniture, electronics, clothing, and personal items damaged by fire, smoke, or soot
  • Smoke damage — not just flames; smoke and soot damage to your property is typically covered too
  • Water damage from firefighting — water used to put out the fire
  • Additional living expenses — hotel, meals, and costs while displaced

SECTION 03What It Does NOT Cover

Renters insurance covers your belongings, not the building — that is the landlord's insurance. It also typically excludes fire that was intentionally set by you, and damage from vacancy or neglect. Some high-value items (jewelry, art) may have coverage limits unless separately scheduled.

SECTION 04How to Handle a Fire Claim

  1. Ensure safety — do not re-enter until authorities clear it
  2. Document everything — photos and video of all damage before cleanup
  3. Make an inventory — list damaged belongings with approximate values
  4. Report promptly — contact your insurer as soon as possible
  5. Keep receipts — for temporary housing and essentials (loss-of-use reimbursement)
  6. Get professional restoration — for smoke and soot damage documentation

This is general information — check your specific policy for your coverage limits and deductible.
