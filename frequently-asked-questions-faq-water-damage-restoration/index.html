Answers to the most common questions about water damage restoration from Allied Restoration Company's IICRC-certified experts.

First, ensure the water source is stopped if possible — locate and turn off the main water shutoff valve. Then call Allied Restoration at (415) 529-5637 immediately. While waiting for our team to arrive, move valuables and electronics to dry areas, do not use electrical appliances in flooded areas, and take photos of the damage for your insurance claim. Do not attempt to use a household vacuum to remove water — this is dangerous and ineffective.
Mold can begin growing within 24 to 72 hours of a water intrusion event. This is why immediate response is critical. The longer water sits in walls, floors, and structural cavities, the higher the likelihood of mold growth. Allied Restoration's emergency team works to begin the drying process as quickly as possible to prevent mold from becoming a secondary problem.
Most homeowner's insurance policies cover sudden and accidental water damage — such as a burst pipe, washing machine overflow, or ice dam — but typically do not cover gradual leaks or flooding from rising water (which requires separate flood insurance). Allied Restoration works directly with your insurance carrier and uses Xactimate software to submit documentation in the format insurers expect, which speeds up the claims process significantly.
The drying phase typically takes 3–5 days for a standard residential water damage event, depending on the extent of saturation and the building materials affected. We monitor moisture levels daily and present readings to your insurance carrier. After drying is complete, reconstruction (replacing drywall, flooring, painting, etc.) varies by scope but typically takes 1–4 weeks for most residential losses.
While you can mop up surface water and run household fans, this is almost never sufficient to properly dry structural assemblies like walls, subfloors, and ceiling cavities. Household fans do not create the air pressure differential or dehumidification capacity needed to dry these materials. More importantly, without professional moisture meters, you have no way to know whether the structure has actually dried to an acceptable level — which means undetected moisture will lead to mold growth.
The IICRC (Institute of Inspection, Cleaning and Restoration Certification) is the world's most widely recognized standard-setting and certification body in the restoration industry. IICRC certified firms and technicians have demonstrated technical competency, ethical business practices, and commitment to ongoing education. When you hire an IICRC certified firm like Allied Restoration, you know the work will be performed to industry standards — which also matters for insurance claims, as many carriers require IICRC-certified contractors.
Our technicians use a combination of calibrated moisture meters (both pin-type and non-invasive), thermal imaging cameras, and physical inspection to map moisture throughout the affected area. We document all readings and create a drying log that tracks moisture levels from initial response through completion. This documentation is provided to your insurance carrier as part of the claim.
The IICRC classifies water damage by contamination level. Category 1 is clean water (from a supply line), Category 2 is gray water (from dishwashers, washing machines), and Category 3 is black water — the most contaminated category, including sewage, flooding from rivers or the ocean, and standing water that has become heavily contaminated. Category 3 water damage requires full personal protective equipment, specialized disinfection protocols, and typically complete removal and replacement of porous materials that were contacted.
